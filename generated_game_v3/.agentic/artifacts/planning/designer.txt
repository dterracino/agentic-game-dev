## Critique of "make a Qix clone"

That brief is too vague to build from. Specifically:

- **No defined loop.** Qix has three interacting systems (drawing, claiming, evading) — which one is the *core* tension you're optimizing for? Without deciding, you'll clone the shell and lose the feel.
- **No decision space.** "Draw lines to claim territory" isn't a decision unless there's a real cost to drawing (time exposed, path length, direction commitment). Otherwise players just nibble safely and the game is boring.
- **No escalation plan.** Original Qix escalates via speed and a stalk/fuse mechanic. Say explicitly what gets harder and how the player *feels* that.
- **No feedback spec.** "Beep boop" arcade audio does real work in the original — tension rising, release on capture. If you don't design that, the clone feels dead even if the algorithm is correct.
- **No scope guardrails.** Polygon-fill enemies, multiple enemy types, and level editors can balloon fast. One developer needs a hard cut list.

Below is a concrete redesign that answers all of this.

---

## The Pitch
**Claim 75% of a shrinking field before the ink runs out or the Qix catches your line. Fast claims are safe but small; bold claims are big but slow to draw and lethal if cut off.**

## Core 30-Second Loop
1. Stand on the safe border.
2. Push into open space — you're now drawing a line ("stix") and are killable.
3. Either finish quickly (short claim, low risk, low area) or run a long diagonal (big claim, more exposure).
4. Reconnect to the border → territory fills, area% updates, short satisfying "claim" beat.
5. Repeat, reading Qix's position and Sparx patrol timing to pick the next cut.

That's the whole game. No mode switches, no menus mid-round.

## Controls (readable, 4 inputs total)
- **Arrow keys / WASD:** move your head.
- Moving **along the claimed border** = safe walking.
- Moving **into unclaimed space** = automatically drawing (no separate "draw" button — removes a decision that adds no value).
- **Shift (optional, cut candidate):** "fast draw" — moves 1.5x speed but you can't turn for the next N frames. This is the *one* skill knob worth keeping because it creates a genuine risk/reward moment (commit to a straight fast cut vs. a slower steerable one).

No mouse, no combo inputs. If a control doesn't map to a decision, cut it.

## Meaningful Decisions
- **Cut length vs. exposure time.** Longer cuts claim more area but leave your line vulnerable longer.
- **Cut angle vs. Qix trajectory.** Qix bounces in your open territory; reading its path to time a cut is the main skill test.
- **Sparx timing.** Sparx patrol the border in both directions; you must time your *launch point* so a Sparx doesn't reach your line's anchor while you're mid-draw.
- **Greedy vs. safe route home.** You can often finish a claim two ways — a short safe return or continuing further to grab a second easy chunk before closing. That's your risk lever, not a UI toggle.

## Enemies & Fair Escalation
Keep exactly two enemy types — no more:
- **Qix:** erratic bouncing polygon confined to unclaimed space. Speed and turn-frequency increase per level (not hitbox size — keep it fair and readable).
- **Sparx (2, then 3 at higher levels):** patrol the claimed border at increasing speed. They never enter open space, so their threat is always legible.

Escalation is two numeric dials only: Qix speed/turn-rate, Sparx count/speed. Add a **soft timer pressure** instead of a hard fuse: every 20 seconds uncleared, spawn one extra Sparx (caps at 4). This nudges pace without adding a new mechanic to learn.

## Feedback (all synthesized, no assets)
- **Visual:** vector line draw with a glowing trail (canvas shadowBlur, additive color), border pulses gold on completed claim, filled area animates in via quick scale-in tween, red flash + camera shake on death, Qix rendered as a jittering wireframe polygon whose color shifts hue with speed (reads as "getting dangerous").
- **Audio (Web Audio oscillators only):** continuous low sawtooth hum for Qix that rises in pitch with its speed (ambient tension gauge); a rising square-wave tone while you draw, pitch tied to your exposed line length (audible risk meter); a satisfying descending arpeggio + noise burst on successful claim; sharp noise burst + hum cutout on death. No sample assets — just oscillator/noise nodes with envelopes.

## Scope for One Developer
- **Data model:** playfield grid as boolean claimed/unclaimed cells or a polygon boundary list; claim resolution via flood-fill from field edges to find enclosed unclaimed region, area via cell count (simpler and safer than polygon math for one dev).
- **Rendering:** canvas 2D, no sprites — everything is stroked/filled shapes and glow.
- **Levels:** one rectangular field, no obstacles, difficulty = the two enemy dials + area% target ratcheting up slightly (65% → 80% over levels). Skip level editors, skip multiple field shapes for v1.
- **Replayability hooks (cheap):** daily seed (fixed Qix RNG seed per day) + local leaderboard on time-to-clear and largest single claim. This gives replay value without new systems.

## Explicit Cut List (protect the scope)
- No power-ups.
- No multiple Qix.
- No level editor or non-rectangular fields (post-launch stretch only).
- No mouse/touch drawing — keyboard-grid only.
- No music track — ambient hum *is* the music.

This keeps the whole game to: one grid/fill system, two enemy AIs, one input scheme, and a synthesized audio/visual feedback layer — buildable and polishable by one person, while preserving the real Qix tension: **commit to a cut, and live with the exposure.**
